Abstract

Microexpressions (MEs) are brief (40–500 ms), involuntary facial movements that reveal suppressed or concealed emotions. Existing ME datasets require high-speed cameras, expert FACS annotation, and tightly controlled laboratory conditions. We present BlendME, the first VR-native microexpression dataset captured using Meta Quest Pro face tracking, recording 70 blendshape activations at 45 Hz from 30 participants under a suppression paradigm.

We introduce a Temporal Residual Microactivation (TRM) pipeline to detect ME candidates from blendshape time series, and a retrospective self-annotation interface with avatar replay that replaces expert FACS coding. After quality filtering, the dataset yields 112 high-confidence ME clips from 21 participants across six basic emotions. Cross-dataset FACS AU validation against CASME II, SAMM, and SMIC confirms emotion-specific correspondence, with within-dataset separability (d′ = 0.96) exceeding established benchmarks (d′ = 0.72). The dataset, annotation tools, and translation pipeline will be released upon publication at swethasree-sribabu.github.io/BlendME.

Dataset

BlendME was collected using Meta Quest Pro face tracking at 45 Hz. Participants viewed 14 validated emotion-elicitation stimuli targeting six emotions under a suppression paradigm. Blendshape time series were captured continuously alongside head pose, confidence scores, and phase labels. After the session, participants annotated detected activation peaks via a retrospective avatar-replay interface at a desktop workstation.

The retrospective annotation interface displays TRM-detected activation peaks on a timeline alongside the stimulus video and an avatar replaying the participant’s recorded blendshape activations. Participants assign an emotion label from 11 categories and an intensity rating for each peak.

Temporal Residual Microactivation (TRM)

The Quest Pro applies internal smoothing that attenuates brief (40–500 ms) facial movements. TRM recovers these attenuated signals by analyzing how rapidly blendshape values change over time, operating independently on each of the 63 facial channels.

  1. 1.Baseline subtractionSubtract per-participant resting state (mean during 60 s neutral baseline) from every frame, producing a deviation signal d(t).
  2. 2.Temporal derivativesCompute velocity, acceleration, and jerk. MEs produce sharp acceleration spikes due to rapid onset, typically reaching peak intensity within 100 ms.
  3. 3.Bandpass filteringButterworth bandpass filter (2–25 Hz) isolates ME-frequency signals. The 2 Hz cutoff removes slow voluntary expressions; 25 Hz removes sensor noise.
  4. 4.Temporal energyRMS over a 150 ms sliding window measures sustained activity. High energy indicates ME-speed movement over the preceding 150 ms.
  5. 5.Event detectionAdaptive threshold at 2 SD above per-channel mean energy. Peaks expanded to onset/offset and duration-gated to 40–500 ms.

Results

Emotion separability. BlendME achieves d′ = 0.96 on emotion-discriminative channels, exceeding the combined CASME II/SAMM/SMIC benchmark of d′ = 0.72. All-channel separability drops to d′ = 0.18 due to a hardware-level BrowLowerer artifact from headset pressure; removing this shared component recovers the discriminative result.

FACS AU correspondence. Spearman rank correlation of mean AU activation vectors between BlendME and each reference dataset. Disgust achieves ρ = +0.717 (p = 0.002) against CASME II ground-truth FACS annotations, confirming faithful capture of the canonical AU9/AU10 pattern. All per-dataset averages are positive.
